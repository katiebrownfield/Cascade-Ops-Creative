Phase 1

Foundation
before you build anything

Week 1 · ~6 hours total · Do this before touching Canva
This phase is the part most people skip because it feels like "not doing the real thing yet." It is the real thing. Every decision you make here determines whether you waste 3 months building the wrong product for the wrong people at the wrong price.
✓
Step 1.1
Decide your exact niche — write it in one sentence
▾

You are not selling "social media templates." You are selling a specific solution for a specific person. The more specific you are, the more your buyer feels like you made this for them. That feeling is what closes sales.

Based on your background, your best niche is: Social media content systems for service-based small business owners — people who have a business (cleaning, notary, contractor, consultant, real estate agent) but have no idea what to post or how to sound professional online.

Your niche sentence
"I make done-for-you social media templates for small service business owners who want to look professional online without hiring a designer or spending hours figuring out what to post."
1
Write your niche sentence in a notebook or notes app. Don't move on until you've written it down.
✓
2
Say it out loud. If it sounds vague or like it could be for everyone, tighten it. "Small business owners" is fine. "People who want better content" is not.
✓
3
Ask yourself: do I actually know this person's problems? You run a notary business. You've managed 6 direct reports. You know what service business owners struggle to communicate. That's a yes.
✓
✓

▾

1

✓

Click the 3 listings with the most reviews. Read every review. Write down exactly what words buyers use to describe what they loved or what was missing. These words are your future marketing copy.
✓

✓
5

✓
6
Write down the price range you see for bundles of 20+ templates. You'll likely see $9–47. Note that top sellers with credibility charge $47–97.
✓
What you're looking for
Shops with 500–5,000 listings of the same type = proven demand. Shops with 10,000+ reviews = the market is real but competitive. Your job is not to beat them on volume — it's to win on specificity and credibility.

Step 1.4
Create your brand identity in Canva — colors, fonts, logo
▾

You need a consistent visual identity across all your products. This doesn't mean hiring a designer. It means picking 3 colors and 2 fonts and using them on everything. Consistency reads as professionalism.

1
In Canva, click your account name → "Brand Kit" → "Add new brand kit." Name it your business name.
✓
2
Pick 3 brand colors. Recommendation for professional credibility: one dark neutral (navy, charcoal, or deep forest green), one warm accent (gold, warm rust, or terracotta), and white. Avoid pastels if your buyer is a business professional.
✓
3
Pick 2 fonts. One serif (like Playfair Display or Lora) for headlines. One clean sans-serif (like DM Sans or Jost) for body text. Add both to your Brand Kit.
✓
4
Create a simple logo. In Canva, search "logo" in templates. Pick one that's text-based (just your business name in your brand font). Keep it simple. You can refine later — don't spend more than 30 minutes on this.
✓
5
Download your logo as a PNG (transparent background). Upload it to your Canva Brand Kit. Now every template you build can use it with one click.
✓

Step 2.1
Build your master template in Canva — the one design system for all 30
▾

Don't build 30 different designs. Build one design system — a consistent visual framework — and apply it 30 ways. This is how professional template sellers produce at speed without sacrificing quality.

1
In Canva, create a new design. Set size to 1080×1080 px. This is your "master" layout.
✓
2
Design a clean, editable layout: a background (solid color or simple pattern using only your 3 brand colors), a clear space for the headline, a space for body text, your logo in the corner. That's it. No decorative clutter.
✓
3
CRITICAL: Use only free Canva elements (or confirm you have a Pro license for any premium ones). If you use a Pro element and your buyer doesn't have Canva Pro, they can't edit it. This causes bad reviews. Stick to free elements unless you explicitly say "Canva Pro required."
✓
4
Duplicate this master layout 29 times (right-click the page → "Duplicate page" in Canva). You now have 30 pages, all with the same structure. Each one will become a different template.
✓
5
Work through each page. Change the headline, change the copy placeholder text, change one visual element to match the post type. Don't redesign from scratch each time — just vary the content within your system.
✓
6
Label each page clearly — "01 - Meet the Team," "02 - Client Testimonial," etc. Buyers will see these names when they open the template. Clarity = 5-star reviews.
✓
Speed tip
Target 4–6 templates per hour. At that pace, 30 templates takes 6–8 hours of focused work. Break it across 3 days: 10 templates Monday, 10 Wednesday, 10 Friday.
✓
Step 2.2
Create the vertical (1080×1350) and Story (1080×1920) versions
▾

Once your 30 square templates are done, resize them for the other two formats. Canva makes this almost automatic.

1
In Canva, with your 30-page design open, click "Resize" in the top bar (requires Canva Pro). Enter 1080×1350 for vertical. Click "Copy and resize." Canva creates a new file — your layouts will need minor adjustment but the system is intact.
✓
2
Repeat for 1080×1920 Stories. For Stories, add a bit more breathing room at the top and bottom since phones display them with UI overlays in those areas.
✓
3
Spend 1–2 hours adjusting text sizes and spacing in the new sizes. Don't redo the designs — just make sure nothing is cut off and text is readable.
✓

You now have 3 Canva files: Square (30 pages), Vertical (30 pages), Story (30 pages) = 90 templates total. This is what you're selling.

Step 2.3
Create your delivery file — the PDF that links buyers to your templates
▾

Here's the slightly weird part about selling Canva templates: you can't upload a Canva file directly. Instead, you generate a "share link" and put it in a PDF. When buyers download your PDF, they click the link, which copies your template into their own Canva account.

1
In Canva, open your Square templates file. Click "Share" (top right) → "Template link" → "Create template link." Copy the link. This is a special link that lets buyers duplicate — not edit — your original. Their edits stay in their copy, not yours.
✓
2
Repeat for the Vertical file and the Story file. You'll have 3 links total.
✓
3
Create a new Canva design — any size, it'll be a PDF. Design a simple "Welcome" document with: your logo, the product name, a brief "How to use" paragraph (3–4 sentences explaining how to click the link), then the 3 clickable links labeled clearly (Square Templates, Vertical Templates, Story Templates).
✓
4
Download this welcome document as a PDF. This is your delivery file. When someone buys from you, they get this PDF, they click the links, done.
✓
Test it yourself first
Before selling anything, click your own template links from a different browser where you're NOT logged into Canva. Make sure the link works and the templates open correctly. If it doesn't work for you, it won't work for your buyers.
✓

Step 4.1
Set up your weekly rhythm — the exact schedule that keeps this manageable
▾

You have a job search, a notary business, and a life. This business will die if it requires heroic effort every week. Here is the minimum effective dose — the least you can do while still growing.

Mon
Monday, 30 min: Batch-create 5 Pinterest pins in Canva. Schedule them to post through the week using Pinterest's built-in scheduler (free).
✓
Wed
Wednesday, 30 min: Write and post 1 LinkedIn post. Respond to all comments from Monday's pin activity.
✓
Fri
Friday, 20 min: Check Etsy stats and Gumroad sales. Note which listings got views. Respond to any buyer messages within 24 hours (this protects your seller rating).
✓
Sat
Saturday, 1–2 hrs (every other week): Build new templates or create a new product. This is your growth time. Don't do it every week — sustainable pace wins.
✓
The rule
If you miss a week, come back and do one pin and one LinkedIn post. Don't try to "catch up" — just continue from where you are. Momentum matters more than perfection.

Step 4.3
Optimize what's working — the monthly 30-minute review
▾

Once a month, spend 30 minutes doing this review. It tells you where to put more energy and where to stop wasting it.

1
Etsy Stats: In your Etsy shop, go to Stats. Which listings got the most views? Which converted (views → sales)? If a listing gets views but no sales, your images or price need work. If it gets no views, your SEO title/tags need work.
✓
2
Pinterest Analytics: In your Pinterest business account, go to Analytics → Overview. Which pins got the most outbound clicks? Make 3 more pins that look similar to your top performer.
✓
3
Kit (email): How many new subscribers this month? What's the open rate on email 3 (the paid pitch)? If it's below 20%, rewrite the subject line. If it's above 20%, leave it alone.
✓
4
LinkedIn: Which post got the most views and reactions? Write a similar one next month. Which post got nothing? Note the format or topic and don't repeat it.
✓
5
Write down your month's revenue in a spreadsheet: Gumroad + Etsy + any direct sales. Track this every month. Watching the number grow is motivating. Watching it stall tells you something needs to change.
